What the Film Shows

Across 7 charted games, Chenal averaged 4.6 tackles per game overall — but that number is almost meaningless without the role split. As a WLB (4 games), he averaged 4.8 tackles per game with 5 big plays (3 TFL, 2 sacks). As an LOLB (3 games), he averaged 4.3 tackles per game with zero big plays. The tackle volume looks nearly identical. The big-play production is not — every single TFL and sack came in the WLB role.

His underlying skills are real. A 5% missed tackle rate against a position average of 18% is elite-level reliability. His film at Wk9 Buffalo (69% box, 7 tackles, 1 TFL, 1 sack) is a genuine preview of what a full-time WLB role looks like on a run-first team.

The liabilities are real too. An 86% catch rate allowed is a coverage problem any new staff will identify immediately. Tackle completion sits at 57% vs 71% position average. And 54 pass rush snaps produced only 3 pressures — dead weight.

The LOLB paradox is the most important data point. His LOLB box rate (57%) was actually higher than his WLB box rate (45%) — yet zero big plays. It's not alignment. It's assignment. The WLB role keeps him in run fits where all 5 big plays lived.
